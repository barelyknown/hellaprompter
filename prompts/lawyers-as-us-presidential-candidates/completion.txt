# Professional Backgrounds of U.S. Major-Party Presidential Candidates (Lawyers vs. Non-Lawyers)

## Overview of Lawyer vs. Non-Lawyer Candidates  
Historically, a **majority of U.S. major-party presidential nominees** have come from the legal profession. In fact, over half of all U.S. presidents (26 out of 45) were trained as lawyers ([The Long History of America‚Äôs Lawyer Presidents | Blog | Lawline](https://blog.lawline.com/the-long-history-of-americas-lawyer-presidents#:~:text=Many%20U,presidents)), and roughly 60% of major-party nominees in history had legal backgrounds (either as attorneys or judges). By contrast, a significant minority have come from **non-legal backgrounds** ‚Äì including military generals, businessmen, and others. Out of **63 individuals** who have been Democratic or Republican presidential nominees through 2020, **36 were lawyers** by profession (57%) and 27 were not. When counting each nomination (including candidates who ran multiple times), about **61% of all major-party candidacies** were led by lawyers. 

- **Early dominance of lawyers:** In the early 19th century, virtually *all* major American politicians were lawyers. For example, founding-era presidents like Thomas Jefferson and James Monroe studied and practiced law before entering politics, as did Andrew Jackson and James K. Polk in the Jacksonian era ([The Long History of America‚Äôs Lawyer Presidents | Blog | Lawline](https://blog.lawline.com/the-long-history-of-americas-lawyer-presidents#:~:text=Many%20U,presidents)) ([Which U.S. Presidents Were Lawyers? - Civille](https://getciville.com/which-u-s-presidents-were-lawyers/#:~:text=Which%20U,John%20Tyler%20%C2%B7%20James)). This trend carried into the formation of the Democratic Party in the 1830s ‚Äì *every* Democratic nominee from 1828 through 1852 had a legal background (Jackson, Van Buren, Polk, Lewis Cass, Franklin Pierce, etc.). 

- **Changing mix over time:** As the country evolved, *non-lawyer* candidates began to appear. Military heroes became political contenders (e.g. **Gen. Winfield Scott** and **Gen. Ulysses S. Grant**), and by the late 19th and early 20th centuries a few businessmen and career politicians without law degrees also made bids. Still, through the **19th century** and into the early **20th century**, lawyers remained dominant among nominees of both major parties. **27 U.S. presidents had worked as lawyers** (and 9 had been career generals) by the mid-20th century ([List of presidents of the United States by previous experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_presidents_of_the_United_States_by_previous_experience#:~:text=List%20of%20presidents%20of%20the,presidents%20previously%20served%20as)), reflecting how common a legal career was for national leaders.

## Party Differences in Candidate Backgrounds  
When comparing the **Democratic vs. Republican** track records, clear differences emerge in different eras:

- **19th century:** Both parties (Democrats, and the Whigs/Republicans) favored lawyers. The Democrats especially were led by a **‚Äúlawyer-politician‚Äù class** (e.g. every Democratic president from 1828 to 1860 was an attorne ([Which U.S. Presidents Were Lawyers? - Civille](https://getciville.com/which-u-s-presidents-were-lawyers/#:~:text=Which%20U,John%20Tyler%20%C2%B7%20James))„Äë). Republicans (founded in the 1850s) also leaned that way initially ‚Äì their first successful candidate, **Lincoln**, was famously a lawyer. Thus, in the 1800s there wasn‚Äôt a stark partisan split on professions; being a lawyer was seen as the standard qualification for statesmanship in either party.

- **Early 20th century:** Still **no strong partisan divide** ‚Äì both parties ran many lawyers, with a few exceptions in each. For example, in 1924 the Democrats nominated John W. Davis (a Wall Street lawyer) while Republicans had Calvin Coolidge (a lawyer ([List of presidents of the United States by previous experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_presidents_of_the_United_States_by_previous_experience#:~:text=List%20of%20presidents%20of%20the,presidents%20previously%20served%20as))„Äë; in 1928, by contrast, Democrat Al Smith and Republican Herbert Hoover were both non-lawyers. Each party was willing to nominate a non-lawyer under the right circumstances, but lawyers were common on both tickets.

- **Late 20th century (post-1980):** A **partisan divergence** took hold. The **Democratic Party became much more likely to choose lawyers** ‚Äì often individuals who built careers in law and then politics (e.g. Mondale, Dukakis, Clinton, Obama, Hillary Clinton, Biden all had legal careers). In these decades, *being a lawyer became almost a prerequisite for Democratic nominees*, perhaps reflecting the party‚Äôs emphasis on public service credentials and policy expertise. Meanwhile, the **Republican Party more frequently turned to non-lawyers**, tapping candidates with military service or business/outsider backgrounds (Reagan, the Bushes, McCain, Trump). Each party‚Äôs strategy has worked in some elections and failed in others.

## Conclusions and Historical Reflections  
Throughout U.S. history, **lawyers have been a mainstay of presidential politics**, especially in the republic‚Äôs first century. The early **‚Äúlawyer-statesman‚Äù archetype** set a lasting precedent ‚Äì even today, many candidates hold law degrees as a badge of credibility. Over time, however, the appeal of *alternative career paths* grew. By the 20th century, Americans proved willing to elect engineers, soldiers, businessmen, and actors to the nation‚Äôs highest office. The **proportion of lawyer candidates has fluctuated by decade**, generally **declining in the mid-20th century** (as war hero generals and others entered the fray) and then **rising again for Democrats** late in the century, even as **Republicans moved the other way**. This divergence after WWII highlights how the two parties responded differently to social currents ‚Äì one valuing institutional experience, the other often valuing outsider status or military leadership.

In terms of outcomes, *being a lawyer vs. not* has not decisively determined who wins the White House. **Lawyer candidates have won many elections ‚Äì but so have non-lawyers**, especially when their personal story or the national mood favored an outsider. What is clear is that each era of U.S. history brought its own expectations for leadership. In the **19th century**, a grounding in law was seen as essential for navigating the young nation‚Äôs legal framewor ([The Long History of America‚Äôs Lawyer Presidents | Blog | Lawline](https://blog.lawline.com/the-long-history-of-americas-lawyer-presidents#:~:text=Many%20U,presidents))„Äë. In the **20th century**, crises like World War II and the Cold War elevated candidates with military or executive skill ([Dwight D. Eisenhower | Biography, Cold War, Presidency, & Facts](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Dwight-D-Eisenhower#:~:text=Dwight%20D.%20Eisenhower%20,Europe%20during%20World%20War%20II))„Äë. In the **21st century**, communication skills and outsider credibility (even reality TV fame) can propel a candidate without traditional credential ([History of Donald Trump | Humans - Vocal Media](https://vocal.media/humans/history-of-donald-trump#:~:text=History%20of%20Donald%20Trump%20,States%20from%202017%20to%202021))„Äë. The major parties‚Äô nominees reflect these shifting priorities. 

To summarize: **Democratic and Republican presidential candidates have evolved from almost uniformly lawyers in the 1800s to a mix of professions in recent decades.** Democrats today still lean into the tradition of the lawyer-politician (as seen with nominees like Obama and Biden), while Republicans have often broken from it (opting for candidates like Reagan and Trump). The success of any candidate, lawyer or not, has ultimately hinged on the person and the moment ‚Äì not just their profession. The dynamic trends in candidates‚Äô backgrounds offer a fascinating window into American political history, showing how each generation reinterprets the qualities deemed necessary to lead the nation.
